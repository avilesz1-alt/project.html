Real Artists vs. The Machine

URL
Disrupting the system
Site Author
 NPR


Abstract
 This site covers the ways in which artists have begun to fight back against the unlawful training of A.I. models without the proper compensation or credit. Much like the Stanford site, this begins by stating that many people have started to take AI companies to court, as well as speaking at government hearings, all for the purpose of bringing justice to real artists whose art has been stolen. Aside from using the law, there are others who have created new ways to disrupt the algorithms training, such as Ben Zhao and his project called Nightshade,or Spawning.ai and its resource, Kudurru. Though there are doubts that these tools will hold up against the ever-changing advancements in technology, artists like Kelly McKernan are thrilled to give A.I. a run for its money. The site concludes with the input from an actual developer of A.I., saying that companies should agree to remove an artist from their algorithms, unfrontunely in most cases, these pleas fall upon deaf ears.



URL
 Visual artists fighting back
Site Author
 PBS
Abstract
 Whereas many of the sites had minimal interviews and quotes from those being directly affected by A.I., this one provides an in-depth look into their thoughts and opinions, as well as what theyve had to go through behind the scenes to make things right. In the beginning, the site gives more information about Kelly McKernan, the artist who was mentioned in the previous abstract. After a deep discussion of their struggles so far, including being low on rent because of the constant theft, McKernan now partners with other artists to sue Stability AI, the people behind Stability Diffusion, for violating the rights of not only the three of them, but a plethora of other creatives, too. The site then goes on to discuss Christoph Schuhumann, the man responsible for a gigantic database that trains A.I., who isnt the least bit remorseful for whats been done. However, that statement is soon combatted by the defiance of McKernan, who believes real art is worth fighting for.
